Streamer Diplomacy: Sentiment Analysis of Global Live Chat Reactions

Author

Zerong Lu

Published

January 1, 2025

1 Executive Summary

This project examines how global audiences react to China when exposed through the livestreams of American mega-streamer IShowSpeed. Using YouTube live chat data from streams in Shanghai and Chongqing, the study investigates whether livestream chats represent chaotic online noise or meaningful patterns of perception. Analyzing 2,000 chat comments through sentiment analysis and keyword exploration, the findings show that most reactions are neutral and observational, with positive sentiment consistently outweighing negative sentiment. These patterns suggest that livestream audiences behave as digital tourists, observing unfamiliar places rather than engaging in political judgment. —

2 Introduction — A Livestream, a City, and Millions of Viewers

Imagine seeing a city you have never visited, not through a documentary or the evening news, but through the shaky camera of a young man walking its streets, reacting in real time, surrounded by crowds, lights, and noise. At the same time, thousands of viewers from around the world are typing short messages—expressing surprise, confusion, excitement, or simply describing what they see. This is the environment of contemporary livestreaming.

IShowSpeed is a popular American internet streamer known for his energetic, spontaneous style and large global audience. While he is primarily an entertainment figure, his livestreams often attract millions of viewers who follow him not only for humor, but also to witness unexpected situations unfold live. In late 2024, IShowSpeed traveled to China and livestreamed his experiences in major cities such as Shanghai and Chongqing.

For many viewers, these livestreams offered a rare and direct visual encounter with Chinese cities. Unlike news coverage or curated travel content, the streams were long, unedited, and unscripted. Viewers saw everyday urban scenes: streets, buildings, transportation systems, crowds, and city lights—often for the first time. As the streamer moved through the city, the audience reacted instantly through live chat.

At first glance, livestream chat messages appear chaotic. They scroll quickly, are often repetitive, and mix jokes, emojis, and short comments. Because of this, live chat is frequently dismissed as meaningless online noise. However, this project starts from a different assumption: that even within this apparent disorder, patterns of attention, perception, and interpretation can emerge.

This study treats livestream chat as a form of collective observation. Viewers use short messages to describe what they see (“this looks modern”), compare it to familiar places (“this reminds me of Korea”), express surprise, or simply react emotionally. While individual messages may be brief or trivial, taken together they form a collective response to a shared visual experience.

The central question of this project is therefore simple but important:

  • When global audiences watch a livestream from an unfamiliar place, are their reactions just noise—or do they reveal meaningful patterns in how places are perceived?

To explore this question, this project analyzes live chat comments from IShowSpeed’s livestreams in Shanghai and Chongqing. Using sentiment analysis and keyword exploration, the study examines how viewers react emotionally and what aspects of the cities they focus on. By comparing two different urban contexts, the analysis asks whether audience perceptions change depending on what they see—or whether similar interpretive patterns appear regardless of location.

By telling a story through data, this project shows that livestream chats are more than background clutter. They are part of how contemporary digital publics encounter, interpret, and make sense of the world in real time.

He’s just 11 years old, messing around with a camera and uploading simple gameplay videos of games like NBA 2K to a brand-new YouTube channel. His real name is Darren Jason Watkins Jr., but the internet would soon know him as IShowSpeed — or just Speed for short.

For the first few years, things moved slowly. Darren was a kid who loved basketball, gaming, and expressing himself loudly. He posted occasionally, gained a small following, but nothing huge. Then came 2021 — the year everything exploded.

Speed started doing live streams where he played games with zero filter. When things went wrong (which was often), he didn’t hold back: he screamed at the top of his lungs, raged dramatically, jumped around like crazy, barked like a dog in memes, and reacted in the most over-the-top ways imaginable. Fans captured these wild moments and turned them into hilarious short clips that spread like wildfire on TikTok and Twitter (now X). People couldn’t stop watching — some were shocked, others were laughing so hard they cried, but everyone talked about him.

Subscribers poured in at an insane rate. From almost unknown, he hit 1 million subscribers in a flash. Then 10 million. Then 20, 30, 40 million… By late December 2025, his main YouTube channel boasts over 46 million subscribers and billions of total views (more than 6.4 billion!). He’s become one of the fastest-growing and most-watched English-speaking streamers ever, winning big awards like Streamer of the Year at the Streamer Awards in both 2024 and 2025.

But Speed didn’t stay in his bedroom forever. His streams evolved into massive real-life adventures. He traveled the world on epic tours — Europe, Southeast Asia, Argentina (for his 20th birthday in January 2025), and more. He’s a huge Cristiano Ronaldo fan, so he’s played in charity soccer matches (like the Sidemen Charity Match and Match for Hope), met football legends, and even jumped into WWE events, including a surprise appearance in the 2025 Royal Rumble.

He’s released music too — songs like “World Cup,” “Shake,” and “Bailar” — and his net worth is estimated between $10–30 million in 2025 from YouTube earnings, sponsorships, merch, and more.

The real magic? Speed’s unfiltered, 100% authentic energy. He screams, dances, cries, laughs, and turns every moment into viral gold. Fans mob him in the streets, and his streams feel like hanging out with your wildest friend.

Millions watched live, and for many, it was their first real, unedited glimpse of modern China through the eyes of this loud, excited American kid.

That’s Speed: from quiet beginnings to global phenomenon. His story proves that sometimes, being completely yourself — even if it’s chaotic and over-the-top — can take you further than anyone ever imagined.

4 How We Did It – Methods at a Glance

Now that we know who Speed is and why his China streams matter, let’s pull back the curtain a little: how do you turn thousands of fast-scrolling, emoji-filled live chat messages into meaningful insights?

The process is surprisingly straightforward — think of it as cleaning up a noisy room so you can actually hear the conversation. We focused on the two main cities from Speed’s early 2025 China tour: Shanghai and Chongqing. For each livestream, we collected around 1,000 real viewer comments (totaling more than 2,000 across both), right from YouTube’s live chat.

Here’s the step-by-step journey we took:

  1. Collecting the raw voices: Using a tool called YoutubeCommentDownloader (or similar YouTube API access), we scraped the live chat comments posted during the streams. These are the exact messages viewers typed in real time — full of excitement, questions, jokes, emojis, and sometimes just random reactions.

  2. Cleaning the mess: Live chat is full of noise: URLs, tons of emojis, repeated words like “the” or “and”, typos, and slang. We used Python and the NLTK library to clean it up — removing links, stripping punctuation, converting everything to lowercase, and filtering out common “stop words” that don’t add much meaning. This left us with clean text ready for analysis.

  3. Finding the feelings and focus:

    • Sentiment analysis: We used VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner), a tool specially designed for social media and short texts. VADER scores each comment as positive, neutral, or negative — even handling things like exclamation points and slang pretty well.
    • Keywords & patterns: We counted the most common words and created word clouds to see what topics jumped out (e.g., “city”, “beautiful”, “modern”, “Korea”).
    • Comparison: By doing the same steps for both Shanghai and Chongqing, we could spot similarities and differences in how viewers reacted to each city.

That’s the toolkit in a nutshell — no fancy AI magic, just careful steps to turn chaotic chat into clear patterns.

Next, we start the real adventure: first stop, Shanghai — the futuristic neon metropolis that had everyone talking.

5 First Stop: Shanghai – The Neon Metropolis

March 24, 2025. After a long flight, Speed steps off the plane in Shanghai — jet-lagged, hyped, and ready to go live. What follows is over six hours of pure, unfiltered chaos: he dives into the streets, tries KFC (rating it a solid 9/10), eats dumplings, plays intense ping-pong bets with locals, gets mobbed by polite but excited fans, visits temples, does backflips in parks, and marvels at the endless sea of skyscrapers and glowing lights.

For millions watching live, this wasn’t just entertainment — it was their first real, raw look at one of the world’s most futuristic cities. The chat exploded.

Viewers were in awe of Shanghai’s scale and sci-fi vibe. Comments poured in about the towering buildings, neon signs, massive highways, and that famous Pudong skyline that looks like something out of a movie.

6 Second Stop: Chongqing – The 8D Cyberpunk Wonder

After the dazzling, sky-high spectacle of Shanghai, Speed continued his China adventure and arrived in Chongqing — a city often called the “8D” or “mountain city” because of its wild, multi-layered geography. Rivers, hills, bridges, and buildings stack on top of each other in a way that feels like stepping into a real-life cyberpunk video game.

The livestream here was just as long and chaotic: Speed tried spicy hotpot (sweating and screaming the whole time), rode crazy escalators that go through buildings, explored night markets, interacted with even bigger crowds, and kept staring in disbelief at the glowing, overlapping cityscape that changes every time you turn a corner.

While Shanghai made people compare and benchmark (“this looks like Korea!”), Chongqing hit differently. The chat shifted from framing and reference to pure, almost childlike wonder. Viewers typed things like “beautiful”, “insane”, “city”, “lights”, “layers”, and “wow” over and over.

Here are two breathtaking views of Chongqing’s legendary nightscape — the exact kind of layered, glowing chaos that blew up the live chat:

  • The word cloud from Chongqing comments shows this clear change in focus. Compared to Shanghai, the comparisons (especially “Korea”) almost disappear. Instead, words about beauty, scale, and the unique vertical feel take over.

The top 10 keywords chart reinforces the difference — while “china” and “streams” still lead (as they did everywhere), the heavy focus on “korea” drops way down, and words like “beautiful” and “city” climb higher.

The emotional tone stayed very similar to Shanghai — mostly calm observation with a lot of positivity. The sentiment distribution for Chongqing looks almost identical:

  • Around positive comments showing excitement and admiration
  • A large majority neutral — people simply describing the unreal views (“this is crazy”, “so many layers”)
  • A small number negative (usually about heat, crowds, or minor stream issues)

The compound sentiment score distribution also mirrors Shanghai’s pattern — a huge neutral spike at 0.00, with gentle tails of positive wonder on one side and minor negativity on the other.

Chongqing felt like the moment viewers stopped trying to “place” the experience on a map and just let themselves be amazed by something truly unique. The contrast with Shanghai was clear: one city invited comparison and global benchmarking, the other invited surrender to pure visual magic.

7 Shanghai vs Chongqing – Spotting the Differences

Now that we’ve explored both cities through Speed’s lens and the eyes of the global chat, let’s put them side by side. What happens when the same loud, energetic streamer shows the world two very different Chinese metropolises? Do viewers react the same way… or does each city tell its own story?

7.1 The Similarities – A Shared “Digital Tourist” Vibe

The emotional tone stayed remarkably consistent across both Shanghai and Chongqing.

Here are the sentiment distributions compared side by side:

Both charts show the same pattern: - A huge peak of neutral comments (~55–60% in both cities) — people mostly describing and observing rather than strongly judging. - A solid amount of positive reactions (~25–30%) full of wonder, excitement, and admiration (“beautiful”, “insane”, “modern”). - A small slice of negative comments (~10–15%) — usually minor gripes about crowds, heat, or stream issues.

The compound sentiment score histograms tell the same story: a massive spike right at neutral (0.00), with gentle positive tails and almost no deep negativity.

This tells us something powerful: no matter how futuristic or layered the city looked, most viewers approached it the same way — as curious observers, not critics. They were “digital tourists” collectively taking notes on a new world.

7.2 The Differences – Each City Left Its Own Mark

While the overall mood was similar, what people talked about changed dramatically depending on what they saw.

Shanghai invited constant comparison:

  • Keywords like “korea” , “modern” , “future” , and “skyscrapers” dominated.
  • Viewers kept trying to place Shanghai in a global context (“this reminds me of Korea”, “looks like Tokyo but bigger”).
  • It was about benchmarking — “Where does this fit in the world I already know?”

Chongqing invited surrender to wonder:

  • “korea” almost disappeared from the top keywords.
  • Words like “beautiful”, “city”, “layers”, “insane”, and “wow”took center stage.
  • The chat focused on the sheer visual uniqueness — no need to compare, just pure amazement at the stacked, cyberpunk reality.

7.3 What This Means

The data reveals two truths at once:

  1. Consistency — Livestream audiences tend to observe first and judge second. Curiosity and neutral description win over negativity almost everywhere.
  2. Sensitivity to place — Even in the same country, different cities produce different conversations. Architecture, scale, and atmosphere shape what people notice and how they frame it.

Shanghai asked “How does this compare to what I know?”

Chongqing simply said “Wow… this is something else.”

Speed’s chaotic tour didn’t just entertain — it turned millions of people into real-time global witnesses, each city leaving its own fingerprint on the collective chat.

8 Conclusion & Looking Ahead

And so the journey through Speed’s China livestreams comes full circle. What began as chaotic, high-energy streams — a young American streamer running through unfamiliar streets, screaming about food and backflips — quietly turned into something much bigger: a real-time window for millions of people to see modern China for the first time.

8.1 The Big Picture: What the Data Really Shows

Across both Shanghai and Chongqing, the live chat told a surprisingly consistent story:

  • Curiosity > Criticism Viewers acted mostly as digital tourists — observing, describing, and marveling rather than judging harshly. Neutral comments dominated (~55–60% in both cities), positive wonder came in a strong second (~25–30%), and outright negativity stayed low (~10–15%).

  • Places shape perception Even though the overall emotional tone was similar, each city left its own fingerprint:

    • Shanghai sparked global comparisons (“looks like Korea”, “so modern”, “future city”).
    • Chongqing inspired pure awe (“beautiful”, “insane layers”, “cyberpunk”).

This shows that livestream audiences are sensitive to visual and cultural differences — they don’t just see “China”; they see different Chinas depending on what appears on screen.

  • Livestreams as bridges In an age of filtered news, edited travel vlogs, and polarized media, Speed’s unscripted, hours-long broadcasts offered something rare: raw, unfiltered access. The chat became a collective voice — thousands of people from around the world quietly reacting, comparing, wondering, and connecting in real time.

In the end, the data proves that even in the middle of barking memes, fan mobs, and spicy hotpot reactions, something meaningful was happening: global perception was being shaped, one comment at a time.

8.2 Looking Ahead – Where This Project Can Go Next

This analysis was just a first step. There are exciting ways to build on it:

  • More cities — Extend the analysis to Beijing, Chengdu, Guangzhou, or even compare China streams with Speed’s other tours (Europe, Southeast Asia, etc.).

  • Temporal patterns — Track how sentiment changed hour by hour during a single stream (did people get more excited at night?).

  • Multilingual support — Many comments mixed English, Chinese, Indonesian, Korean — future versions could handle non-English text better.

  • Improved tools — VADER does great with social media, but it sometimes misses sarcasm, streaming slang (“santai”, “sniped”), or context. Adding more advanced NLP models could refine the results.

  • Deeper topics — Use advanced topic modeling or emotion detection to uncover subtler themes (humor, respect, cultural surprise).
